What you are looking at

a simplified transformer, running live

This is a small, illustrative model of how a language model processes text. It is not a real network running inference, but every mechanism shown is structurally faithful to a real transformer block.

The lattice. The six stacked grids are weight matrices, one per layer. These are the learned parameters: billions of numbers in a real model. Color and height encode each weight value.

The flux. Tokens enter as a structured input sequence. Each one carries a small latent vector, an embedding. As a token crosses a layer it is transformed by that layer's weights, so its state and color change at every step. Switch on FOCUS to follow one token and watch its representation evolve across the six layers.

Attention. At each layer a token does not act alone. It looks at the other tokens in that layer through query and key projections, attends most to those whose keys match its query, and blends in their values. Three heads read different relationships, shown as three colors of links and selectable in the heatmap.

Quantization. The FP32 to INT4 buttons change how many bits represent each weight. Fewer bits means less memory and faster compute, but a coarser model: the weight histogram collapses, and the range of possible outputs shrinks with it.

Sampling. At the output, the final state becomes a distribution over next-token bins. Temperature reshapes it: low temperature concentrates the choice, high temperature spreads it, which you can see in the next-token chart and in the color variety of exiting tokens.

Memory. The KV cache grows with the number of tokens in context, which is why long contexts cost memory. The per-layer bars show where that cache currently sits.

Scale is reduced for clarity: a six-dimensional latent instead of thousands, fixed projections instead of trained ones, and memory and throughput figures that are proportional rather than measured.
